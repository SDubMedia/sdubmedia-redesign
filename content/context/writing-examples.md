# Writing Examples — Geoff Southworth / SDub Media

The blog agent should read these before writing any post. These examples show the tone,
sentence rhythm, and personality to match. Adapt the voice to video production — don't
copy the real estate subject matter.

**Source:** realestateinfoguide.com (Geoff's writing, pulled 2026-05-24)

---

## What These Examples Show About Voice

- **Practical and direct.** No fluff. Gets to the point without talking down to the reader.
- **People-first framing.** The value to the reader comes before the sales pitch.
- **Balanced — acknowledges the hard stuff.** Doesn't pretend everything is easy. This builds trust.
- **Accessible.** Doesn't assume the reader is an expert. Explains things simply without being condescending.
- **Concrete examples over abstract claims.** "Power washing can make a huge difference" not "improve your home's exterior appeal."
- **Short, scannable structure.** Headers, short paragraphs, digestible chunks.

---

## Example 1 — Practical, honest framing

> "Owning a home is a significant milestone in many people's lives. It represents stability,
> security, and a sense of achievement. But it also comes with real responsibilities — ongoing
> maintenance, unexpected costs, and a time commitment that catches a lot of first-time owners
> off guard."

**Why this works:** Leads with the good, but doesn't hide the hard part. Readers trust you
more when you acknowledge what's difficult.

**Apply to video:** Same approach for "what does a video production shoot actually involve?"
Lead with the value, then be honest about prep time, turnaround, what clients need to bring.

---

## Example 2 — Concrete, specific, not vague

> "Power washing can make a huge difference, especially for siding, decks, driveways, and
> walkways. Neutral tones like grays, beiges, and whites give a timeless look — but test paint
> colors in different lighting before committing."

**Why this works:** Specific. Actionable. Tells you exactly what to do, not just "improve
your curb appeal." No filler.

**Apply to video:** Don't write "great video helps your business grow." Write "a 90-second
brand video posted every week adds up to 52 touchpoints with your audience over a year —
each one building familiarity before a prospect ever calls you."

---

## Example 3 — Accessible, non-jargon explanation

> "Each mortgage payment increases your ownership stake. Over time, this builds financial
> security in a way renting simply can't replicate. And unlike stocks, real estate is something
> you can actually use while it appreciates."

**Why this works:** Explains a concept clearly without assuming the reader knows anything.
Draws a comparison that makes the abstract concrete.

**Apply to video:** "Most businesses think of video as a one-time expense. It's not. A well-made
brand video keeps working — on your website, in your proposals, on LinkedIn — for two to three
years. The cost per impression drops every month it's live."

---

## What to Avoid

- Don't open with "In today's fast-paced world..." or any version of that.
- Don't use: leverage, utilize, delve, elevate, game-changing, unlock, transform.
- Don't write four-bullet-point paragraphs back to back. Vary the structure.
- Don't be vague. If you make a claim, back it up with a number or a specific example.
- Don't write for Google. Write for a Nashville business owner sitting in their office deciding
  whether to spend money on video.

---

## The Reader

A Nashville business owner — real estate team, school, corporate brand, service company.
They're busy, a little skeptical of marketing spend, and want to know if this actually works.
They're not looking for inspiration. They're looking for a straight answer.

Write like you're explaining it to them over coffee, not presenting to a boardroom.
