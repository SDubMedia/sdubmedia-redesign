Agents tend to obsess over which camera body to buy, when the truth is that for real estate, the body matters less than almost anything else. Once you clear a basic bar, the [lens](/blog/2026-05-25-best-lens-for-real-estate-photography/), the light, and the technique do far more for your listing photos than spending another thousand dollars on a fancier camera. Here's how to choose the best camera for real estate photography without overpaying for features you'll never use.

## What Actually Matters for Listings

Real estate photography asks very little of a camera compared to, say, sports or wildlife. You're shooting still rooms, usually on a tripod, with time to set up each frame. That means the specs people pay premiums for, blazing autofocus and high frames-per-second, are nearly irrelevant here.

What does matter:

- **Good dynamic range.** Interiors have a hard problem: the room is darker than the bright windows. A camera with good dynamic range captures both the room and the view outside in one frame, or makes it easy to blend a couple of exposures together. This is the single most useful trait for real estate.
- **Interchangeable lenses.** So you can mount the wide-angle that does the real work.
- **Manual controls.** You'll want to set exposure deliberately, not leave it to auto.
- **A tilting screen.** Helpful for low and high angles without contorting yourself.

That's the whole list. Almost any modern mirrorless camera checks these boxes.

## What You Can Ignore

- High megapixel counts. Listing photos are viewed on phones and laptops; 24 megapixels is plenty.
- Fast continuous shooting. Rooms don't move.
- The newest model. Last year's body, or a used one, shoots identical listing photos for less.

## Solid Picks by Budget

Rather than name models that go out of date, shop by tier:

- **Entry-level:** any current entry mirrorless body from the major brands, paired with a wide-angle, will produce professional-looking listings. This is where most agents should land.
- **Mid-range:** a step up buys you better dynamic range and low-light performance, which helps with dim interiors and twilight shots. Worth it if you shoot listings constantly.
- **Pro:** full-frame bodies give the best dynamic range and the widest lens options, but the gap between mid-range and pro is small for real estate specifically. Buy here only if you're shooting high-end homes daily.

Put your money toward the lens and a tripod before the body. A mid-tier camera with a great wide-angle beats a pro body with a kit lens every time.

## Or Start With Your Phone

If buying a camera feels like a big leap, you don't have to make it yet. A modern phone shoots usable listing photos, and you can upgrade later once you're sure photography is part of your routine. See [iPhone real estate photography](/blog/2026-05-25-iphone-real-estate-photography/) for how far the phone gets you and when it's time to graduate. Either way, the [core real estate photography tips](/blog/2026-05-25-real-estate-photography-tips/) apply to whatever you shoot with.

## The Bottom Line

Clear the basic bar (good dynamic range, interchangeable lenses, manual controls), spend the savings on glass and a tripod, and skip the spec race. The camera is the least important variable in a great listing photo.
