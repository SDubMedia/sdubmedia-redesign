Most businesses don't need "a video." They need a specific thing for a specific reason, and they don't have the vocabulary to ask for it. So they call a production company, say "we want to do some video," and get a quote that may or may not match what's actually in their head.

That gap is what this post fixes. When people search for video production services in Nashville, they usually find a list of companies, not a list of services. They still don't know whether they need a brand film or a testimonial, a one-day shoot or a monthly retainer, a full crew or one good operator. So here's the honest breakdown: the services that actually exist, what each one is for, what it tends to cost, and how to tell which one you need.

We do this work, so we'll be specific. But the goal here is to make you a smarter buyer, not to sell you a package.

## The Building Blocks, Not the Buzzwords

Almost everything a Nashville production company offers is some combination of a handful of core services. Once you can name them, every quote starts making sense.

Think of it less as a menu and more as a set of building blocks. A "brand campaign" is really a brand film plus social cutdowns plus maybe a few testimonials. An "event package" is event coverage plus a highlight edit. Knowing the pieces lets you ask for exactly what you want instead of buying a bundle that's 30 percent stuff you'll never use.

Let's go through the pieces one at a time.

## Brand Films and "Who We Are" Videos

This is the anchor video most businesses picture first. It's the polished, two to three minute piece that lives on your homepage, plays at the top of a proposal, or runs at a conference. It explains who you are, what you do, and why anyone should care, usually with a mix of interviews, b-roll of your team and space, and a clear narrative.

A good brand film is the most "produced" thing most businesses ever commission. It often needs scripting, a second camera, real lighting, and clean audio, which is why it sits higher on the price scale. In Nashville, a polished brand film usually runs somewhere from $4,000 to $12,000, depending on crew size, number of locations, and how much direction it needs.

Here's the honest part: a brand film is worth it when it has a clear job. The homepage anchor. The piece you build everything else around. Where it goes wrong is when a business treats it as their entire video plan, spends the whole budget on one shoot, and then has nothing for the other 51 weeks of the year. One great film is one touchpoint. It matters, but it's a starting line, not a finish.

## Testimonial and Case Study Videos

A testimonial video is one of the highest-return services on this list, and one of the most underrated. It's a customer or client on camera, in their own words, explaining what working with you was actually like. Nothing sells like a real person who has no reason to lie for you.

The production itself is relatively simple: one location, a comfortable interview setup, good audio, clean lighting. The hard part isn't the camera. It's getting a real person to relax and say something genuine instead of reciting a stiff endorsement. That's a craft, and it's the difference between a testimonial people believe and one they scroll past.

Because the setup is lean, testimonials usually fall on the lower end of the cost range, often $1,500 to $4,000 for a single shoot, less per video if you film several in one day. If you want the full playbook on getting these right, we wrote a whole guide on [how to get great testimonial videos for your Nashville business](/blog/2026-05-24-testimonial-video-production-nashville/). It covers prepping the person, the questions that actually work, and the mistakes that make a testimonial feel fake.

## Event Coverage and Live Video

If you're hosting a conference, a gala, a fundraiser, or a company-wide meeting, event video captures it: the speakers, the room, the energy, the moments you can't recreate later. The deliverable is usually a highlight recap (a couple of minutes that capture the feel of the day) and sometimes full recordings of keynotes or sessions.

Event work has one rule that nothing else on this list does: there are no second takes. The keynote happens once. The applause happens once. If the audio fails or the crew misses the moment, it's gone. That's why event coverage rewards experience and planning over everything else, and why booking early matters more here than anywhere.

We saw the upside of doing this well with Ballet Ball Nashville. We had full backstage access and filmed the dancers performing from multiple angles, then captured the whole atmosphere of the night: the entrance, the detail shots, guests dancing, the energy of a live room. The brief was to deliver both the performance and the feel of the event in one recap, and that blend is what good event coverage is really about.

## Recurring and Series Content

This is the service most businesses don't know to ask for, and it's the one that fits the most of them. Instead of one big shoot, recurring content is video produced on a regular cadence: a weekly series, ongoing social clips, a monthly feature. The relationship is a retainer, not a project.

The reason it works is simple math. A 90-second piece posted every week is 52 touchpoints a year, each one building a little more familiarity before a prospect ever calls. A single film, however polished, is one touchpoint that slowly ages on a page. The businesses winning attention online are the ones that show up consistently, not the ones with one perfect video from two years ago.

Pricing here works differently, which trips people up. Instead of a per-project fee, it's usually a monthly retainer, often $1,500 to $6,000 depending on volume. That can sound steep until you do the math: with a steady setup you might walk away with four to eight pieces a month, and the per-video cost often drops below what you'd pay booking those shoots one at a time. You also skip the start-from-scratch tax every single shoot.

This is the model we built SDub around. Our clearest example is Coldwell Banker Southern Realty: eight offices across Tennessee, a weekly series filmed with the owner at the main Nashville office, monthly headshot days where agents rotate through, and ongoing content produced in their in-house podcast room. It's not one shoot a year. It's a steady drumbeat across several formats, and that consistency is exactly why it works.

## Headshots and Photography Days

Not strictly "video," but it's a service plenty of production companies offer alongside it, and it pairs naturally with recurring work. The setup is straightforward: a clean, consistent backdrop and lighting, and people cycle through to get a professional headshot.

What makes it valuable for a business is consistency across a team. When everyone's headshots match (same lighting, same look), your website and your LinkedIn presence feel intentional instead of cobbled together from whatever selfies people had. For a growing company that's always adding staff, a recurring headshot day solves an annoying problem for good.

## Podcast Production

Podcasts have quietly become one of the most useful content formats for businesses, and producing one is a real service: the room setup, multiple cameras, clean multi-track audio, and the editing that turns a long conversation into a watchable episode plus short clips for social.

The clip part matters more than the full episode for a lot of businesses. One recorded conversation can become a dozen short, vertical clips that feed your social feeds for weeks. That's recurring content with a single day of filming behind it, which is part of why podcast rooms have become a fixture in the recurring-content world.

## Voiceover and Audio

Audio is the service nobody asks for by name and everybody notices when it's missing. Amateur video gives itself away through sound before picture: muddy, echoey, hollow audio reads as cheap even when the footage looks fine.

Good companies obsess over this. A small example from our own work: when we shot a brand video for Tutoring Rocks in Nolensville, we recorded the voiceover in a closet, because the clothes absorb echo and gave us clean sound without a treated room. Small, unglamorous choices like that are a big part of what separates watchable video from amateur video.

## How to Tell Which Services You Actually Need

You don't need all of this. You need the right two or three things for your situation. A few honest guidelines.

If you have a clear, one-time need (a homepage anchor, a recruiting video, a conference recap), buy that specific service and don't let anyone upsell you into a bundle you won't use. If your real goal is visibility and staying top of mind, stop thinking in single projects and start thinking about cadence: a recurring setup will almost always serve you better than one big shoot.
